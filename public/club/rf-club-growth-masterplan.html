01 — The club today

What you have built is
already exceptional

Before diagnosing the gaps, it's important to understand what the club already is — and why it matters. Most companies spend years trying to build what you have.

⬡

A curated, trusted community

1,000+ manually vetted members. Every single one went through an onboarding call before being accepted. This is not a Slack workspace anyone can join — it is an exclusive network. That trust and curation is the club's most valuable asset and cannot be replicated quickly.

🤝

Real relationships with real people

The club has active members and genuine relationships. People from major web3 companies — protocols, DAOs, exchanges — are inside. When they meet at a physical event, they go deep. This is not a LinkedIn group full of passive followers. It's a network where trust is already established.

📍

Physical events that already work

Physical events are profitable through sponsors. That means the community is cohesive enough to attract external sponsorship — a signal of credibility and reach. Most community efforts never reach financial sustainability. You already have it.

"The Web3 Finance Club is not a marketing asset that needs to be built from scratch. It is a living, trusted network of exactly the people Request Finance needs to reach — that is already generating 32% of all leads. The job is not to create it. The job is to engineer it."

The two pillars — what the club already does

Pillar 1

Acquisition engine

The club brings new, qualified people into the RF orbit. The funnel: website traffic → application → onboarding call → acceptance into the club. Once inside, members are exposed to RF naturally through events, conversations, and the community context. This is working — it generates 32% of leads. But it's not being actively fed or optimised.

Pillar 2

Conversion engine

Club members are invited to a demo call about Request Finance. The rep notes confirm this is "quite working" — people who join the club and attend a demo convert at meaningfully higher rates than cold outbound. But the demo invitation process is informal, inconsistent, and not tracked as a funnel. This is your highest-leverage conversion lever and it is not being run as one.

03 — The diagnosis

What's working.
What's broken.

An honest audit of the club's current state — so the manager and CMO can align on what to fix and in what order.

✓ What's working
The vetting and onboarding call
Manual vetting + an onboarding call before joining is a differentiator. It creates a sense of exclusivity and pre-filters for genuine interest. Don't remove it — scale it.
Physical events with sponsors
Profitable events signal community legitimacy. The ADAN dinner proved the format works. Keep the physical-first mentality — it produces the strongest relationships.
The demo invitation pipeline
Members invited to RF demos convert well. The mechanism works. It just needs to be systematised, tracked, and scaled.
The depth of existing member relationships
Simran and the club manager have genuine relationships with members. This is rare and precious. Members who feel known stay active and refer others.
✗ What's broken
No activity between events
The club has no regular programming, no weekly touchpoints, no content calendar, no online forum activity. Members join and then hear nothing for weeks. A community that goes quiet loses its members — not through formal departures, but through disengagement.
No conversion funnel tracking
There is no record of which members have been invited to demos, which attended, which converted to RF customers, and what the conversion rate is. Without measurement there is no optimisation.
No member acquisition engine
New members come in organically and through sales rep referrals. There is no systematic marketing targeting the new TAM (web2 finance + stablecoin-curious people). The club is not growing with intention.
The RF connection is too implicit
Members join the Web3 Finance Club — not a Request Finance community. The bridge from "I'm in this club" to "I should try RF" is not built. Most members do not connect the dots without a nudge.
One manager for everything
One person cannot simultaneously run member acquisition, onboarding calls, event logistics, content creation, online community management, and demo coordination. Without a clear priority stack, everything is done at surface level.

The onboarding call — keep it, but structure it

The onboarding call is a differentiator — don't remove it. But it needs a consistent structure so the manager can run 5–8 per week without burning out and so every new member gets the same quality experience.

Onboarding call structure (30 minutes)

  1. Minutes 0–5 — Welcome and context. Who runs the club, what it is, what it isn't. RF connection made explicit: "The club is run by Request Finance — we're a payments platform for web3 companies. The club exists to connect the finance leaders in this space."
  2. Minutes 5–15 — Learn about the member. Their company, role, biggest pain points in managing crypto payments or treasury. This is the discovery call RF never gets with cold prospects.
  3. Minutes 15–25 — What the club offers. Events, community, access to peers, the newsletter, the online forum. What they can contribute. Ask: "Who else in your network should be in this club?"
  4. Minutes 25–30 — The bridge to RF. "Given what you've shared, we have a product that might be directly relevant — happy to set up a separate 20-minute demo if useful." Soft offer. Not a sales pitch. Plant the seed.

What to capture in CRM after every onboarding call

  • Company type: Web3-native / web2 going crypto / stablecoin-curious
  • Role: CFO / Finance Manager / Treasury / Ops
  • Current payment tools: What they use today
  • Pain points mentioned: The exact language they use
  • RF demo interest: Hot / warm / cold
  • Referrals mentioned: Who else they think should join
  • Company size and stage
  • Source: How they heard about the club
💡
Why this matters: This data becomes the club's conversion intelligence. It tells you exactly when and how to send the RF demo invitation — based on what each member said they need, not a generic blast.

07 — Pillar 2: Member conversion

Turning members into
RF customers

The mechanism exists — members are invited to RF demos and it works. The job is to transform this from an informal practice into a systematic, measured conversion funnel with clear stages, triggers, and handoffs.

The conversion journey — 5 stages

Stage 1: Member accepted 1,000 members today
↓ First-week welcome sequence + community onboarding
Stage 2: Activated member Posts, attends event, or replies to content
↓ 30 days after joining — personalised demo invitation
Stage 3: Demo invited ~30% of active members — target
↓ Demo attended — CSM handoff to trial
Stage 4: Trial started ~50% of demo attendees — target
↓ KYB + onboarding + first payment
Stage 5: Paid customer ~35% of trial users — target
What makes stage 2→3 convert

The demo invitation must be personalised and timed. Not a newsletter blast. A message from the club manager referencing something specific the member shared in their onboarding call or in the community. "Given that you mentioned paying contractors in Ukraine — I thought this 20-minute overview of how other club members handle it would be useful."

What makes stage 3→4 convert

The demo must be tailored to the member's use case — not a generic product walkthrough. Club members are warm leads. They don't need to be sold the concept. They need to see their specific problem solved. The demo should open with "Based on what I know about your setup, here's what matters most for you."

What makes stage 4→5 convert

KYB friction kills club-sourced deals at the same rate as cold ones. A fast-track KYB path for club members — the same concept as the partner program's fast-track — would meaningfully lift this conversion rate. The club manager should have a contact at compliance to escalate club member KYBs.

08 — The demo funnel

Engineering the demo
invitation system

The demo invitation is the most important commercial action the club takes. It should not be ad-hoc. Here is the exact system to build around it.

The demo invitation message

Sent by the club manager, from their personal email or LinkedIn, 30 days after a member joins (or sooner if they've shown high engagement). Three versions — one per member type.

[For web3-native member]
Hi [Name], great having you in the club. Given that [Company] is managing [use case from onboarding call], I thought it was worth seeing how a few other club members have solved this with Request Finance. It's a 20-minute overview — no pitch, just the specific flows that are relevant to your setup. Worth your time?
[For web2 / stablecoin-curious member]
Hi [Name], now that you've been in the club for a few weeks — we've been sharing a lot about how companies like yours are starting to use stablecoins for [their pain: international payments / payroll / vendor payments]. Request Finance is the platform behind a lot of that. Happy to show you a 20-minute walkthrough — no commitment needed.

Trigger rules — when to send

  • Standard trigger: 30 days after joining. By then they've had time to engage with the community and feel part of it before seeing the RF demo pitch.
  • Fast trigger (7 days): If the member mentioned a specific active pain point in the onboarding call ("we need to switch our payment tool right now") or an immediate use case, invite sooner.
  • Event trigger: After every physical event, send demo invitations to all attendees who are not yet RF customers. The event context makes the transition feel natural.
  • Content trigger: If a member comments on or shares RF-related content in the club forum, that's an engagement signal — follow up with a demo invitation within 48 hours.
  • Never send: A generic mass invitation to all 1,000 members at once. This destroys the trust that makes club leads so valuable.
🎯
Target rate: 20 personalised demo invitations per week. At a 40% acceptance rate, that is 8 demos per week from the club — 32 per month — on top of whatever sales reps are booking.

The demo-to-paid pipeline — who owns what

Club manager owns
  • Onboarding call intelligence (pain points, use case, timing)
  • Demo invitation — personalised, timed correctly
  • Relationship warmth — member trusts the club manager, not a stranger
  • Follow-up if no reply to demo invite (1 follow-up max)
  • Feeding intel to the RF sales rep before the demo
Sales rep owns
  • The demo itself — personalised to what the club manager shared
  • Trial setup and KYB initiation
  • Pricing conversation
  • Close
  • Feeding new RF customers back to club manager (they become ambassadors)
CSM owns
  • Trial-to-paid conversion
  • Onboarding and first payment success
  • Reporting new customer status back to club manager
  • Flagging club customer feedback for club programming
  • Renewal conversations — looping in club value as a retention argument
09 — Club activation & programming

Bringing the club
to life between events

A community that only gathers physically once a quarter is not a community — it is an event series. To retain 1,000 members and convert them, there must be value between events. This is the programming architecture.

📰

Weekly newsletter

Every Tuesday · 300 words max

The heartbeat of the club. Every member hears from the club once a week. Short, valuable, never salesy. Written by the club manager with input from the CMO.

Newsletter structure:

  • One insight — a trend in web3 finance this week (2–3 sentences)
  • One member spotlight — what a member is doing (rotates each week)
  • One resource — a tool, article, or regulation update useful to the community
  • One invite — upcoming event or demo invitation (subtle, non-pushy)
🎙️

Monthly online session

First Thursday · 60 minutes

A monthly virtual gathering for the full club. Keeps momentum between physical events. Not a webinar — a conversation. Guest speaker + Q&A. Maximum 60 minutes.

Session formats that work:

  • Peer fireside: One CFO from a club member company shares their finance stack and decisions. No pitch. Real talk.
  • Expert Q&A: A lawyer, regulator, or technologist answers questions on a specific topic (MiCA compliance, stablecoin accounting, DAO treasury structure)
  • RF product preview: Once a quarter, the online session features an RF product preview — new features, roadmap, feedback session. Members feel like insiders.
💬

Active online forum

Telegram or Slack · Always-on

A monitored, curated online space where members can ask questions, share resources, and connect. The club manager posts 3× per week to keep it alive. Structure with channels, not one big noisy group.

Channel structure:

  • #introductions — new member intros (club manager prompts each new member)
  • #stablecoin-payments — questions and tips
  • #regulatory-watch — MiCA, Genius Act, country updates
  • #tools-stack — what people use and recommend
  • #events — upcoming and recaps
  • #opportunities — jobs, partnerships, intros
🤝

Member x member introductions

Bi-weekly · curated by manager

Every two weeks, the club manager makes 3–5 curated introductions between members who would benefit from knowing each other. "Introducing Nacho from Status.im (looking for a stablecoin accounting tool) to Aaron from Regen Financial (fractional CFO who specialises in web3 accounting)." This is the club's most unique value — it delivers business value to members without being about RF at all. And members who get value from the club stay active, attend events, and convert to customers.

🏆

Member recognition programme

Ongoing · low cost · high retention

People stay active in communities where they feel seen. A lightweight recognition programme keeps members engaged without expensive benefits.

  • Member spotlight: Featured in the weekly newsletter once per year
  • Founding member badge: For members who have been in the club for 12+ months
  • Top contributor: Monthly recognition for whoever added the most value in the forum
  • Speaker priority: Active members get first access to speak at events
  • Referral recognition: Members who bring in accepted referrals are thanked publicly (not just via a commission — via social recognition)
